MicroRNA Data


What is MicroRNA?

MicroRNAs are small, highly conserved non-coding RNA molecules involved in the regulation of gene expression. MicroRNAs are transcribed by RNA polymerases II and III, generating precursors that undergo a series of cleavage events to form mature microRNA. MiRNAs achieve gene regulation by targeting specific messenger RNAs and marking them for degradation or translational repression. Numerous high-profile diseases, such as diabetes, Alzheimer's disease, cardiac hypertrophy, acquired immune deficiency, and numerous cancers are closely associated with miRNA functionality.

How is MicroRNA Used for Cancer Detection?

miRNAs have well studied roles in controlling biological functions such as cellular proliferation, differentiation, and apoptosis, which form the basis of cancer development. In relation to cancer, miRNA expression profilings can serve as a more accurate method of classifying cancer subtypes than proteincoding gene expression profilings. Such classification is enabled by the heterogenous miRNA expression patterns among cancer types, with miRNAs serving as highly tissue specific tumor suppressors and oncogenes. In an effort to better understand such relationships, potential miRNA cancer biomarkers have been studied based on expression profilings with significant incidence in cancerous tissue. These biomarkers and distinct miRNA expressions have enabled to use of machine learning and data science methodologies for cancer detection and cancer subtype distinction.





Connecting microRNA Data and Machine Learning


The difference in miRNA expression among cancer types and the extensive documentation of biomarkers has enabled the development of biologically supported machine learning models. Machine learning algorithms, such as the SVM and random forest models used in this project, exploit and learn patterns present among training data samples to classify future unseen data. However, the patterns learned can be any of the infinitely many present in a dataset. Often, classifiers that perform well focus on seemingly irrelevant features, making it hard to justify usage and inspire confidence in non-machine learning engineers that may use the end product. However, by comparing established miRNA biomarkers with feature selection signatures, we can see the relevance of the models to biologically studied pathways and miRNAs. The models in this application learn the patterns present in the miRNA ID, the miRNA's corresponding read count, and reads per million attributes present in the miRNA expression dataset. Three different tissues datasets were used to create binary cancer classifiers and a single multiclass cancer-tissue differentiation model. As a brief overview into model functionality, a SVM seeks to separate two or more classes using hyperplanes to create decision boundaries that maximize the width of the gap between the classes. A Random Forest model uses the aggregate decision of several decision trees to determine the classification of the datapoint.











Additional Information


The data that we used for this project was obtained from the GDC data portal. The data attributes were miRNA_ID, read_count, reads_per_million_miRNA_mapped, and cross-mapped. However, we removed all features except the reads_per_million_miRNA_mapped and miRNA_ID so that the models could use the concentration of miRNA for each miRNA to create the classification model.
